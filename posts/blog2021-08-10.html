Sapiens: A Brief History of Humankind

Posted on August 10, 2021 by Eila
book review
My copy of Sapiens, by Yuval Noah Harari

A book that has long been on my list

Sapiens came out when I was in university, and has been on my list for years. When I saw it out on the front tables during a Chapters visit, I figured it was finally time to pick up a copy of my own and give it a go. I was not disappointed - overall it was a good read. It started out really strong, and I couldn't put it down. However, the book loses some momentum partway through and it was a bit more effort to fully finish the book.

The book is divided into several parts. I'll try to avoid summarizing all the author's points, but rather provide my opinion on each part of the book.

Part One: The Cognitive Revolution

This was by far my favorite part of the book. I enjoyed reading the wild speculation on the pre-historic era of humankind. That is, pre-agricultural revolution. It was fun to think about what like may have been like when there were multiple species of humans on the planet. A little disturbing, also, to think of the destructive powers of Homo Sapiens, and our part in driving other humans and animals to extinction.

Part Two: The Agricultural Revolution

Seems like Harari is of the opinion that the agricultural revolution was a disaster for humans. As he puts it, it was not us that tamed grains, but rather grains that tamed us. Interesting to think that the average peasant, compared to the average hunter gatherer, had a worse diet and longer working hours as a result of the advent of agriculture. The trade-off, I suppose, was the ability to feed many more humans in a smaller area. There was also a long section here 'debunking' reasons for why throughout history there have been far more patriarchal rather than matriarchal societies. It was at this point that I think the author's style of making bold semi-unbacked claims started to lose steam. In the cognitive revolution section, there is a huge amount of grey area that allows for speculation. However as the book progresses through the ages, using 'pop-culture' factoids to make a point becomes markedly less powerful.

Part Three: The Unification of Humankind

This is where I started to lose steam in the book. I think it simply was less interesting to me, as it focused more on the rise and fall of religions, empires, etc. The section on money was good. His point about money being one of the unifying factors in enabling cultures to work together and trade goods, despite having very different religious belief systems was the best part of this section.

Part Four: The Scientific Revolution

The first half of this section was entirely the author expounding upon why the West was the most successful in conquering the world. I found it interesting that the author tried to entirely attribute the success to be a specific European mindset of "discovering the unknown". It's definitely presented as having been the "right" way to do things, which I think shows the author's bias. Again, as the book progresses the bold semi-backed statements become ever more jarring.

The last half of this section turns again to the question of happiness, and speculates on directions that humanity could take in the future. The speculations are interesting, but again, there's certain generalisations that the author used to emphasize how far science has come, that caused him to lose credibility. For example, in comparing modern-day sex changes to historical eunuchs, he writes:

For instance, we can today not merely castrate a man, but also change his sex through surgical and hormonal treatments.

I would argue that among the surgical treatments he alludes to, castration included, they are mostly cosmetic and do not provide function. As for the new hormonal treatments, they are undoubtedly a huge leap for science and for allowing a person to present as the opposite sex, however they are still a far cry from completely changing a human from one sex to the other. This un-backed statement-as-fact about a field that still has a long way to go was jarring, and I believe there are many more like it in the book. For me, this slides the book towards the light entertainment and speculative end of the universal history genre.

In Summary

Sapiens was a good read, especially for someone like myself who does not often read books on history. It had an extremely ambitious scope, and did a fairly good job of touching on many different milestones in our collective past. However, the book stumbles as it draws closer to the present day. The author's biases become more apparent, and the brief format of the book can't handle the complexities of our modern-day debates. Overall, I give it a 3 out of 5 stars.
